Why This Exists

A Note on How I Read These Frameworks

This is how I see frameworks
translating a solution into reality

However different the discipline — consulting, health innovation, impact investing, implementation science — every framework here is doing one of the same five jobs. It's diagnosing a problem, deciding what to do about it, funding the action, proving it worked, or scaling it so it lasts.

Different vocabulary, same five functions. That's the lens this whole series is built on — and it's also why the fourth notebook below looks and feels different from the rest. Implementation science wasn't built to persuade a client by Friday; it was built to survive a journal reviewer years later. Same map, different instrument.

1
Diagnose
What's actually going on here?
›

Before anything else, the mess gets structured into something checkable. Every discipline here refuses to act on a vibe — they all force the hypothesis onto paper before testing it.

2
Decide
Given the diagnosis, what do we actually do?
›

Almost every decision tool here is a quadrant in disguise. Nobody trusts a single number — everyone collapses competing tensions onto two axes so a room of humans can point at a box and agree.

2×2 Matrix Ansoff Matrix BCG Growth-Share Matrix Hypothesis-Driven Thinking The Pyramid Principle Cost-Benefit Analysis Profitability Framework Market Sizing Stage-Gate Process Health GTM Channel Strategy Capital Stack Selection
3
Fund
Who pays for this, and who's accountable?
›

This is where "good idea" meets "but who's actually going to resource it." Blended finance and the Valley of Death are the same problem — engineering who bears the risk so capital will move — wearing finance and health-tech clothing respectively. Regulatory and IP clearance live here too: they're gatekeeping steps, not technical ones.

The Valley of Death TGA Regulatory Pathway Reimbursement Pathway Mapping IP Strategy for Health Innovation Technology Transfer Process Blended Finance Social Impact Bonds RACI Matrix The Consulting Work Plan Three Horizons of Growth Kübler-Ross Change Curve Value Chain Analysis
4
Prove
Did it work — and would it have happened anyway?
›

The most crowded stage, because causation is the hardest move to defend. The counterfactual question shows up everywhere under a different name — additionality, deadweight, comparator, assumption, CMO configuration — and it's always asking the same plain thing.

Additionality / "But For" Test Five Dimensions of Impact IRIS+ Impact Risk Assessment Impact Due Diligence Process Theory of Change (Investor Lens) HEOR / ICER HTA: PBAC & MSAC SROI Theory of Change Logic Model Kirkpatrick Model Realist Evaluation (CMO) RE-AIM OECD-DAC Criteria Developmental Evaluation
5
Scale
Having proven it, how do we make it last and grow?
›

Scaling frameworks are obsessed with drift — the fear that something proven small will degrade once it's bigger, faster, or run by someone else. Goodhart's Law is basically the thesis statement of this entire stage. This is also where academic implementation science concentrates hardest — its whole discipline exists to answer "does it still work when we're not in the room?"

Scaling Readiness Assessment Balanced Scorecard OKRs KPI Framework Design Clinical Adoption Pathway Impact Portfolio Construction OPIM CFIR i-PARIHS Normalization Process Theory Knowledge-to-Action

Whatever the project, I'm drawn to the same role: translating a good idea trapped in expert language into something someone can actually act on. These five functions are how I think about doing that, at any scale.
